## A. Why Vitamin C is the recommended next category

### Keyword opportunity (Phase 8A)

Phase 8A ranked **Vitamin C** as the #1 new category candidate from Ahrefs exports dated 2026-07-07:

| Signal | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Kept keywords | 9 Tier-1/Tier-2 terms |
| Aggregate traffic potential | ~190k (parent-topic weighted) |
| KD range | Mostly 1–9 |
| Head terms | `best vitamin c supplement` (vol 8,100, TP 49,000, KD 5) |
| Form-specific | `liposomal vitamin c supplement` (vol 700, TP 5,500, KD 2) |
| Absorption angle | `best vitamin c supplement for absorption` (vol 150, TP 53,000, KD 4) |

Vitamin C outscored Vitamin D on immediate traffic-potential fit in this export despite lower raw head volume on some terms.

### Label-comparison fit

Vitamin C supplements vary widely on dimensions SuppCheckr already scores well:

- **Form clarity** — ascorbic acid, sodium ascorbate, calcium ascorbate, liposomal, whole-food blends
- **Dose transparency** — vitamin C amount per serving vs. proprietary blends
- **Ingredient simplicity** — fillers, sweeteners, flavor systems in powders and gummies
- **Third-party testing** — USP, NSF, Informed Choice where applicable
- **Value normalization** — price per 500 mg or 1,000 mg vitamin C
- **Claim conservatism** — immunity, absorption, and "natural" marketing copy

### Amazon monetization fit

Vitamin C has strong Amazon SKU availability across form buckets (capsules, powders, liposomal liquids, buffered formulas). SuppCheckr already has a proven affiliate CTA workflow from Creatine and Magnesium clusters — exact ASIN/UPC matching required before enabling per-product CTAs.

### Manageable claim risk

When framed as **label education** and **intake support** (not disease prevention or treatment), Vitamin C is lower YMYL risk than condition-specific stacks, gray-area longevity compounds, or testosterone-framed minerals. Risk increases sharply if copy drifts into cold/COVID/cancer/whitening/deficiency-treatment framing — guardrails below.

## E. Claim boundaries

### Must not claim

- Prevents or cures colds, flu, or COVID
- Treats cancer or any disease
- "Boosts immunity" in a disease-prevention sense
- Safe for everyone
- Best for kids, pregnancy, or any vulnerable group as universal guidance
- Megadosing is safe without limits
- Kidney stone risk is irrelevant for all readers
- Treats deficiency without clinician involvement
- Liposomal or buffered forms are universally superior

### Preferred wording

- "Supports normal immune function" (with NIH/FDA-appropriate context)
- "Helps meet vitamin C intake needs" / "contributes to daily vitamin C intake"
- "Labels vary by form, dose, additives, and serving size"
- "Some products use ascorbic acid, buffered mineral ascorbates, or liposomal delivery systems"
- "Ask a qualified healthcare professional if pregnant, nursing, under 18, managing kidney disease, prone to kidney stones, taking medication, or managing a medical condition"

### Comparison framing

- Score and compare **labels**, not personal health outcomes
- No "Best Overall" or "Best for Everyone"
- Criterion labels on comparison table = supported label attributes, not condition winners

## F. Source rules

### Recommended source types

| Source | Use for |
|--------|---------|
| [NIH ODS Vitamin C fact sheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminC-Consumer/) | RDAs, UL, food sources, deficiency context (conservative) |
| NIH ODS Health Professional fact sheet | Dose, interactions, clinical background |
| FDA dietary supplement overview | Regulatory framing, structure/function limits |
| FTC endorsement/disclosure guidance | Affiliate/sponsored content compliance |
| USP Verified / NSF / Informed Choice / Informed Sport databases | Third-party testing verification |
| Official brand product pages | Primary label facts |
| Retailer listings | Only when official label unavailable; document fallback |

### Do not use as primary sources

- Amazon reviews, Q&A, ratings, or live prices
- Influencer immunity or absorption claims
- Unsourced "immune boosting" marketing
- Medical blogs or condition-treatment sites
- Animal/pet supplement sources

## G. Scoring rubric draft

Proposed Vitamin C scoring profile weights (total 100):

| Criterion | Weight | Notes |
|-----------|-------:|-------|
| Dose transparency / vitamin C amount clarity | 18 | mg per serving clearly stated; no hidden blends |
| Form clarity | 16 | ascorbic acid, sodium/calcium ascorbate, liposomal, whole-food blend named |
| Label clarity | 14 | serving size, servings per container, allergens |
| Third-party testing transparency | 12 | verified cert vs. marketing-only claim |
| Evidence-aligned claims | 12 | no disease/immunity overclaims |
| Price value (per 500 mg or 1,000 mg vitamin C) | 12 | dated manual price normalization |
| Formula quality / ingredient simplicity | 8 | minimal unnecessary actives |
| Additives / sweeteners | 4 | especially powders and chewables |
| Brand/source transparency | 4 | manufacturer contact, lot/COA access |

**Liposomal/buffered substantiation:** Products making enhanced-absorption or gentler-stomach claims should be scored on whether label facts support the marketed form — not on accepting absorption superiority without conservative framing.
